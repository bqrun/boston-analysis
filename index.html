Reproducing Past Analyses

See http://boston-qualifier-stats.blogspot.com/ and http://www.namethatbostonmarathoncutoff.com/.

First, I tried to reproduce these analyses as closely as possible. The methodology is described in detail here.
The basic idea is as follow:

  1. Get all the finisher data for the top 25 "feeder" marathons (I'm actually using the top 25 for each of the past 3 years)
  2. Count how many finishers from last year met the designated cutoff (2015: 62s, 2016: 147s)
  3. Call this number n
  4. For the current year, order finishers in decreasing order by the margin (in seconds) with which they qualified
  5. Count down to the finisher with the nth largest margin
  6. This person's margin is the estimated cutoff
There are a number of assumptions for this model to be accurate, including all years must have equivalent (1) Boston field size, (2) Total number of qualifiers, (3) Contribution to Boston from these feeder races, and (4) Likelihood of any given qualifier registering for Boston.

The Complicated Way

I wanted to come up with a better way. Most notably, I want to avoid caveats and ignoring individual races (ex. Berlin) by explicitly modeling the relative contributions of each race. I have also run the analysis over past years so that we can actually estimate its accuracy and hopefully improve over the more straightforward analyses above. Here it is.

Importantly, I'm also using a slightly different definition of "qualifier" than the previous analysis. Most everyone reports only those who met the qualifying time for their current age when they ran the qualifying race. In reality, one need only meet the qualifying standard for the age they will be when they run Boston. To approach this number, particularly when I'm trying to figure out which qualifiers actually ran Boston, I allow each finisher to age up to 2 years, accounting for many races run the better part of 2 years before the Boston for which they qualify. For this reason, the number of qualifiers used and shown below will be a little higher across the board than other numbers out there. In the above analysis, this change makes very little difference, but it will impact the registration rates I use below.

So, to estimate a cutoff, I sort all finishers in decreasing order by qualification margin and give each a weight equal to the registration rate for their qualifying race. I then run a cumulative sum down the list until the total is equal to the number of finishers who ran Boston from these races last year. This person's margin represents the predicted cutoff and takes into account registration rate variability between races. If this same method was applied last year, the prediction would have been 138 seconds - not quite 147, but noticeably closer than 91.

From a practical point of view, this is largely because the Boston marathon itself contributes a huge number of qualifiers and has a relatively high registration rate, so this year's rather hot and slow Boston marathon dramatically reduced the expected cutoff.

Pure speculation

The above analysis reduced our original assumptions by exactly one. In reality, there is also unaccounted-for variation in the registration rate per race over time and last year's number of Boston runners is not always an accurate proxy for this year's number of runners from these races. The proportion of the Boston field made up of runners from these top 25 races is not constant and varies according to both the number and speed of qualifiers from these races, and the many smaller qualifying races.

Have some data

I've had many question about how the seemingly high registration rate so far (after the 1st week) might affect the cutoff. Below are plots of the margins of Boston runners and the average registration rate by qualifying margin year over year. Note that, yes, the registration rate is significantly higher among those with smaller qualifying margins. Interestingly, there is a significant registration rate among those who don't meet the cutoff for a given year. This must be because they 1) ran another qualifying race with a faster time - they'd be counted twice in my analysis, or 2) my method for matching names of qualifiers to Boston runners is producing a bunch of false positive matches.

But what does it mean!? It means that the registration rate for the 0-5 min group is, on average, 46% (for those who meet the cutoff), while the registration rate among 5+ min qualifiers is only 32%. We'll assume that this relative rate of registration between 5+ and <5 min qualifiers remains the same. Let's do some math: 

5+ min qualifiers (Q2017): 31,597
5+ min registrants (Q2017): 17,484
5+ min registration rate (Q2016): 0.3231

<5 min qualifiers (Q2017): 10,122
<5 min registration rate (Q2016): 0.4642

17,484 / 31,597 = 0.5533              observed 5+ registrants / observed qualifiers (Q2017)
0.5533 * (0.4642 / 0.3231) = 0.7950   expected <5 registrants / observed qualifiers (Q2017)
10,122 * 0.7950 = 8,047               expected <5 min registrants (Q2017)

(30,000 * 0.8) - 17,484 - 8,047 = -1,531    expected field size (for qualifiers)
                                            - real and expected registrants

1,531 / 0.7950 = 1,926                      estimated excess qualifiers
This is probably too many. 17,484 + 8,047 = 25,531 registrants, which is over 85% of the field. 80% of the field of 30,000 would be 24,000. To get to 24,000, we can count down the field 1,531 expected registrants, equivalent to 1,531 / 0.7950 = 1,926 qualifiers on our list. The bottom 1,926 qualifiers come up to a margin of 53 seconds. Obviously, this is noticeably different (and worse) than the 0 (or -42) seconds I was estimating before registration started.

Why did he mislead us? Why did he give us false hope and now he's taking it away? Because the registration rate among qualifiers does not follow a predictable pattern. This was one of our assumptions way back at the beginning. I showed way above (the first plot) that the registration rate by race changes every year, but does not follow any noticeable pattern. Below is the overall registration rate (from these races) over the past 5 years, and the estimate given the registration so far this year. This indicates that either registration actually increased across the board, or the set of large feeder races we were using as a proxy for all races is not actually representative of all qualifiers. We know that a lot of these qualifiers were hot and slow (read: blocked by a train), so maybe these people went and qualified at smaller marathons. This is one of any number of plausible explanations for the failure of our initial model to predict the true registration rate.

To get this estimate of the registration rate from the total for the 5+ min group (17,484), we have to do some tricky math since this is not directly comparable to either our qualifier set or the registration estimates based on the Boston results list. The 2016 5+ min registration rate was 0.3231. If this stayed the same for 2017, which we assumed, we would have expected to see 31,597 * 0.3231 = 10,209 registrants from this set of qualifiers, and 10,122 * 0.4642 = 4,698 registrants <5 min. Last year, 27,488 of presumably 30,000 registrants ran, or 91.6%. So we would have observed 10,209 + 4,698 = 14,907 of (~24,000 * 0.916) registrants, or 67.8% of the field. This should have been a clue, since (as shown above somewhere), last year these same qualifiers made up only 53% of the field. If these qualifiers make up 67.8% of the field, then the effective registration rate from the qualifiers we observed was about (0.678 * 17,484 = 11,856) / 31,597 = 0.375. This represents the highest registration rate over the 6 years I have data, and a 10.3% increase over last year - enough to account for over 2,000 excess registrants.

New model

We obviously missed at least one critical parameter. The most obvious, that we have the data to rectify, is the variance in registration rate by qualifying margin. As shown above, the closer your qualifying margin, the more likely you are to register. We have all of this data, so it's relatively easy to incorporate it into the model. Our new weighting criteria is as follows: 

qualifer_weight = race_registration_rate * margin_registration_rate / average_registration_rate

We don't want to just compute the registration rate for every combination of race and margin, we just don't have the sampling power to do that without overfitting, hence we compound the two weight factors separately, then normalize by the average registration rate. If we go ahead and use this new weighting scheme to compute the cutoff as above, and with no knowledge of the registration rate so far this year, we get an estimated cutoff of 48 seconds. This is consistent with our estimate of 53 seconds knowing the 5+ registrants this year. Notably, this new model retroactively predicts the 2016 cutoff at 132 seconds, worse than 138, but still within striking distance of 147.
